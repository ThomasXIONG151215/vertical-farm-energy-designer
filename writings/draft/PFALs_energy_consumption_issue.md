Recent extreme climate events and pandemics have underscored vulnerabilities in global food supply chains, prompting a shift toward resilient, localized food production systems[1][2]. This trend has accelerated the adoption of Controlled-Environment Agriculture (CEA), particularly Plant Factories with Artificial Lighting (PFALs), which facilitate year-round, climate-resilient crop production[3][4][5]. PFALs achieve this through precise automation of environmental parameters, including nutrient delivery, temperature, humidity, CO2 concentration, and light intensity, utilizing LED arrays to provide photosynthetic radiation and HVAC systems to maintain ideal growing conditions[6][7][8]. However, the high capital costs of PFALs, ranging from $1200/m2 to $4000/m2 for PFAL with cultivation area under 100 m2, present significant economic barriers[9][10]. Although enhancing production can result in cost reductions of up to 80%, the extent of these savings diminishes as the scale of the PFAL increases[9]. Consequently, prioritizing cost efficiency at the onset of the project is crucial. Operational expenses further exacerbate economic challenges, with energy consumption accounting for at least 30% of total production costs, driven primarily by LED lighting (55-70% of energy use) and HVAC systems (20-30%)[4][5][11][12]. This underscores the energy-intensive nature of PFALs, necessitating research into cost-effective and energy-efficient strategies to ensure their sustainable implementation.
From a theoretical perspective, some researchers studied PFAL parameters through energy modeling and parameter analysis. Liebman-Pelaez et al.[13] modeled PFAL building energy consumption and achieved robust validation (normalized mean bias error of 3%) with urban planning tools. Song et al.[14] integrated measured lettuce growth data into the PFAL model and suggested that air-side economizers could reduce PFAL cooling demand by 3.5-58.3%. Yu et al.[15] further validated the potential of air-side economizers to reduce 33.8% in air-conditioning energy. Bu et al.[16] identified envelope overall heat transfer coefficient, air conditioner coefficient of performance (COP), and light efficacy as the three most important factors for energy-saving. Arcasi et al.[17] compared energy consumption across different locations under optimized configuration. Eaton et al.[18] established ideal concrete energy consumption metrics of 6.2-12.0 kWh per kilogram of lettuce production by simulation. While some studies optimize PFAL design using building energy measures, others explore plant-environment interactions. Choi et al.[19] found that dehumidification accounts for 50% of energy demand in their study case due to plants evapotranspiration. Graamans et al.[20] modeled crop transpiration with 15.96 g/m2/h accuracy. Kim et al.[21] linked nutrient temperature to environmental factors. Nie et al.[22] highlighted impacts of air temperature and light sources. Talbot et al.[23] validated a dynamic crop model with 0.1%–3.5% energy use accuracy. Decardi-Nelson et al.[24] explored the application of advanced machine learning techniques, such as Deep Reinforcement Learning, in PFAL climate control, demonstrating potential energy savings of up to 35%. 
Compared to the modeling work, experimental research mostly focused on component-level energy efficiency optimization as airflow units and LEDs. Sohn et al.[25] optimized airflow uniformity via computational fluid dynamics. Fang et al.[26] modeled lettuce leaves as porous media. Kang et al.[27] improved heat removal with smaller air orifices. Li et al.[28] saved 50.8% of energy using interlayer airflow. Wang et al.[29] cut energy use by 15.8%–73.7% with outdoor air cooling. Zhang et al.[30], Haibo et al.[31] and Perone et al.[32] applied airflow for disease prevention in similar ways. In terms of lighting, Olvera-Gonzalez et al.[33] demonstrated 11.8% energy savings through pulsed LED systems, while Li et al.[34] reduced electricity consumption to 61.2 kWh/m² using movable LED configurations. Complementary approaches included the one of Cai et al.[35] with high-efficiency reflective films yielding a 36% reduction in lighting energy consumption, and the hybrid lighting system developed by Mutombo Arcel et al.[36] that improved energy efficiency from 40.47 to 93.92 g/kWh.
