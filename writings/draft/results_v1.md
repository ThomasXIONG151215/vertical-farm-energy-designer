# Results and Discussion

## 3.1 Model Validation and Verification

The performance of the integrated PV-BES-PFAL system was first validated to ensure the accuracy of subsequent analyses. FigS1 presents the validation results for both the EnergyPlus building energy model and the PV power generation model.

### FigS1: EnergyPlus Model Validation and PV Model Verification

FigS1A shows the validation of the EnergyPlus model's HVAC energy forecast capability during August 2023, comparing predicted AC kWh against measured data with statistical metrics (R² = 0.908, CV(RMSE) = 19.26%, NMSE = 0.026) that confirm the model's accuracy. The time-series plot shows predicted (red line) and measured (blue line) AC electricity usage over a two-week period, with good agreement in both pattern and magnitude between the two datasets. FigS1B visualizes how annual AC energy consumption inside the PFAL varies according to the EnergyPlus model when envelope thermal conductivity and thickness are modified. The heatmap employs a color gradient from blue (low energy consumption, ~2400 kWh) to red (high energy consumption, ~3200 kWh), clearly demonstrating that higher envelope conductivity (y-axis) increases the facility's sensitivity to external climate conditions, thereby increasing HVAC energy requirements, while greater envelope thickness (x-axis) produces the opposite effect, with consumption decreasing as thickness increases.

The PV power generation model validation is presented in FigS1C, which compares our simulated P_max predictions with Jinko Solar manufacturer P_max specifications across different irradiance levels (200-1000 W/m²). The bar chart shows simulated (blue bars) versus manufacturer (red bars) maximum power output, with excellent agreement across all irradiance levels. FigS1D illustrates the relationship between PV power generation, solar irradiance, and ambient temperature through a heatmap visualization, confirming that PV output increases with higher irradiance but decreases as temperature rises. The color gradient from red (~150W) to blue (~650W) shows the combined effect of these factors, with optimal power generation occurring at high irradiance (800-1000 W/m²) and low temperatures (-30 to 0°C).

## 3.2 Climate Characteristics and Energy Requirements

Representative cities from five distinct climate zones in China (Shanghai, Harbin, Haikou, Lasa, and Urumqi) were selected to evaluate system performance across diverse environmental conditions. Fig5 illustrates the key climate characteristics of these cities that influence both PFAL energy demand and PV-BES system generation capacity.

### Fig5: Climate Characteristics and Energy Profiles Across Cities

Fig5A compares the average daily solar radiation across cities using a bar chart with direct numerical values. Haikou shows the highest daily solar radiation (9.50 kWh/m²/day), followed by Lasa (8.86 kWh/m²/day) and Shanghai (8.73 kWh/m²/day), while Harbin has the lowest (7.33 kWh/m²/day). The daily average temperatures presented in Fig5B show significant variation through a bar chart with error bars, with Harbin (~5.5°C) and Urumqi (~8.5°C) having the lowest annual mean temperatures, while Haikou experiences the highest (~25.5°C). The error bars indicate temperature variability, with Shanghai and Haikou showing substantial seasonal variations.

HVAC energy consumption patterns across cities are depicted in Fig5C. This figure compares the daily HVAC energy consumption using a bar chart with error bars indicating uncertainty. The chart reveals that Haikou has the highest daily HVAC requirement (~13 kWh), followed by Shanghai (~10.5 kWh), while Lasa has the lowest (~8.5 kWh). The relatively small error bars demonstrate that annual HVAC energy requirements have low uncertainty. This pattern can be explained by the internal heat generation from PFAL equipment, which reduces heating needs in cold seasons but increases cooling demands in warm climates when combined with external thermal infiltration.

Fig5D displays the diurnal solar radiation patterns across six distinct time periods (00:00-04:00, 04:00-08:00, 08:00-12:00, 12:00-16:00, 16:00-20:00, and 20:00-24:00) for all cities using line plots with different colors for each city. The visualization reveals that all cities experience peak radiation in the 12:00-16:00 period, with Haikou showing the highest peak radiation (~1100 W/m²), followed closely by Lasa and Shanghai. All cities show similar diurnal patterns but with different intensities, with minimal radiation during nighttime hours and rapid increases during morning hours (04:00-08:00).

## 3.3 Techno-economic Performance and Optimization Results

The optimization results for various system configurations across all cities are presented in Fig6, offering insights into the economic viability and technical performance of the integrated system.

### Fig6: Economic Performance and System Sizing Results

Fig6A showcases the optimal levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) achievable for two distinct payback period targets through a grouped bar chart: 0-3 years (blue bars) and 3-5 years (red bars). The chart shows that shorter payback periods (0-3 years) result in higher LCOE values across all locations, ranging from approximately 0.051 $/kWh in Lasa to 0.057 $/kWh in Shanghai. For longer payback periods (3-5 years), LCOE values decrease to between 0.043 $/kWh (Lasa) and 0.052 $/kWh (Shanghai). The error bars indicate the uncertainty in these LCOE estimates, which is relatively small across all scenarios. 

The corresponding optimal system sizing parameters are shown in Fig6B, illustrating how PV array size (bar chart) and battery energy storage (BES) capacity requirements (dotted lines with markers) vary across cities for different payback period targets. The dual-axis chart reveals that for 0-3 year payback periods (blue bars and red diamonds), most cities require PV array sizes of 30-50 m², except for Lasa which needs only about 20 m². For 3-5 year payback periods (green bars and purple diamonds), required PV array sizes increase to 40-80 m², with Harbin requiring the largest array. Battery capacity requirements for the 3-5 year scenario are significantly higher (30-40 kWh) than for the 0-3 year scenario (10-15 kWh), with Haikou showing the highest battery capacity requirement (~40 kWh) for the longer payback period.

When targeting specific LCOE values of $0.04 (blue bars) and $0.06 (red bars), the resultant payback periods vary considerably across locations as shown in Fig6C. The grouped bar chart demonstrates that for the lower LCOE target ($0.04), payback periods range from about 4.85 years (Lasa) to 5.62 years (Harbin). In contrast, for the higher LCOE target ($0.06), payback periods are much shorter, ranging from only 0.34 years (Lasa) to 0.71 years (Harbin). This indicates that achieving more economical electricity production requires significantly longer financial recovery timeframes.

The system configurations necessary to achieve these target LCOE values are illustrated in Fig6D, demonstrating the technical adaptations required across diverse climatic regions. The chart employs bars for PV array size and dotted lines for battery capacity, showing that achieving the lower LCOE target ($0.04) requires substantially larger PV systems (30-80 m²) with larger battery capacities (35-40 kWh) compared to the higher LCOE target ($0.06), which can be achieved with much smaller systems (10-20 m² PV, minimal battery capacity). Shanghai and Harbin require the largest PV arrays (~80 m²) to achieve the $0.04 LCOE target, while Lasa needs the smallest system.

## 3.4 Feasibility Analysis and Design Implications

To assess the potential for near grid-independence, our analysis examined different system configurations across all cities. The results demonstrate that thoughtful system sizing and operational scheduling can significantly enhance the economic performance of integrated PV-BES-PFAL systems without compromising crop production goals, offering an additional optimization dimension beyond hardware specifications. The optimal combinations of location, system sizing, and operational scheduling identified in this study can reduce LCOE by up to 18% compared to baseline configurations, while achieving reasonable payback periods across all studied climate zones.

The five cities examined in this study represent diverse climate conditions across China, providing a robust foundation for generalizing results to similar climatic regions globally. Haikou, with its high solar resource and cooling-dominated load, represents tropical and subtropical regions; Shanghai typifies temperate coastal climates with moderate solar resources; Harbin represents cold northern climates with more limited solar potential; Lasa exemplifies high-altitude regions with excellent solar resources but challenging temperature extremes; and Urumqi represents continental arid climates with good solar resources but significant seasonal variations.

These findings provide valuable decision support for stakeholders by illustrating the technical boundaries of system design across various climatic conditions, enabling targeted investments that balance economic considerations with desired performance outcomes. The results suggest that integrated PV-BES-PFAL systems are economically viable across all studied climate zones, though with varying optimal configurations and performance metrics.
